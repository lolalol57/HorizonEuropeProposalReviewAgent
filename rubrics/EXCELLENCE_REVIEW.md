# Horizon Europe Excellence Review

> **Coverage:** this is a broad-coverage internal review. Emit one findings item for
> every numbered review module below (`na` with a short reason when not applicable).

## 1. Purpose

This file defines the complete review framework for Criterion 1 — Excellence.

The review must assess the proposal against:

- the official call and topic requirements;
- the Type of Action;
- the official Horizon Europe Excellence evaluation questions;
- the proposal's own scientific, technological and methodological claims;
- the quality and measurability of the objectives;
- the ambition beyond the State of the Art;
- the credibility of the proposed R&I developments or innovations;
- the quality of the methodology;
- the maturity and validation logic;
- the internal consistency of the Excellence section;
- the quality and understandability of relevant figures.

The review must not rely on keyword detection alone.

The reviewer must assess:

- quality;
- specificity;
- credibility;
- ambition;
- measurability;
- verifiability;
- advancement;
- methodological coherence;
- traceability;
- internal consistency.

The proposal must be evaluated as submitted.

---

## 2. Inputs

The Excellence Review should use the following inputs where available:

- Call Requirements Map;
- Type of Action;
- Proposal Map;
- extracted Excellence content;
- extracted objectives;
- extracted KPIs;
- extracted baselines and targets;
- extracted Key Technologies;
- extracted Key Research and Innovation Developments;
- extracted Key Innovations;
- extracted TRLs;
- extracted tables;
- extracted figures;
- Claude Vision findings for extracted figures;
- citation and footnote mapping;
- relevant structural findings.

External verification of cited sources is not required by default.

The review should only assess whether:

- important claims have identifiable citations or footnotes;
- important numerical claims are referenced where appropriate;
- externally derived baselines have identifiable sources;
- citation and footnote presentation is sufficiently clear and consistent.

---

## 3. Type of Action Adaptation

The review must adapt to the Type of Action.

### Research and Innovation Action — RIA

For an RIA, place greater emphasis on:

- scientific advancement;
- methodological advancement;
- technological advancement;
- unresolved research or technological uncertainty;
- new knowledge;
- new models;
- new methods;
- new technological capabilities;
- proof of feasibility;
- research validation;
- credible progression from lower maturity levels.

Preferred internal terminology may include:

- Key Research and Innovation Developments;
- Key R&I Advances;
- Research Validation;
- Validation Setting;
- Demonstration Environment;
- Experimental Environment;
- Proof of Feasibility.

A RIA may contain innovations, prototypes and demonstrations.

The word "pilot" should not automatically trigger a negative finding.

However, repeated deployment-oriented or market-ready framing combined with weak
R&I content may indicate an inconsistency with the nature of an RIA.

---

### Innovation Action — IA

For an IA, place greater emphasis on:

- innovation maturity;
- integration;
- operational demonstration;
- measurable practical improvement;
- advanced prototypes;
- system-level validation;
- deployment readiness;
- replication potential;
- credible progression towards operational or market uptake.

Preferred internal terminology may include:

- Key Innovations;
- Operational Demonstration;
- Pilot;
- Large-Scale Pilot;
- Replication Site;
- Deployment Preparation;
- Pre-Commercial Validation.

An IA may still contain research activities.

The review should evaluate the overall balance rather than imposing an artificial
boundary between research and innovation.

---

## 4. Review Status

Use the following internal status system.

### ✅ Adequately Addressed
The proposal addresses the aspect clearly, specifically and with sufficient
supporting information.

### ⚠️ Partially Addressed
The proposal addresses the aspect, but relevant information, evidence, detail,
clarity, consistency or traceability is incomplete.

### ❌ Not Adequately Addressed / Missing
The proposal does not sufficiently address the aspect or the relevant information
cannot be identified.

### ➖ Not Applicable
The aspect is not materially relevant to the proposal, topic or Type of Action.

---

## 5. Internal Improvement Report

For important findings marked:

- ⚠️ Partially Addressed
- ❌ Not Adequately Addressed / Missing

use the following structure where appropriate.

### Evidence from the Proposal
Provide one or more of:

- a short direct quotation;
- section reference;
- page reference;
- table reference;
- figure reference.

Do not quote unnecessarily long passages.

### Reviewer Assessment
Explain:

- what is incomplete;
- what is unclear;
- what is insufficiently justified;
- what is inconsistent;
- why the issue matters for the Excellence criterion.

### Suggested Improvement
Where useful, provide:

- an improved formulation;
- a stronger objective;
- a stronger Key Technology name;
- a stronger Key Innovation name;
- a clearer KPI formulation;
- a clearer traceability structure;
- a better table structure;
- a stronger methodological explanation.

The Final ESR-style comment must not contain rewriting suggestions.

## 6. Opening and First Impression

### 6.1 Opening Hook
Check whether the Excellence section begins with a strong opening.

- ☐ Does the first paragraph immediately communicate the central societal,
  scientific, technological, industrial or environmental problem?
- ☐ Where meaningful, is the magnitude of the problem quantified?

Consider: How much? How many? How often? At what cost? Who is affected? What is
currently lost? What remains unsolved? Why is action needed now?

- ☐ If quantitative framing is not appropriate, does the proposal begin with a
  strong scientific, technological or conceptual problem statement?
- ☐ Does the opening avoid overly generic formulations such as: *"In recent
  years, X has become increasingly important."*
- ☐ Can an evaluator quickly understand: what the problem is; why it matters; why
  existing approaches are insufficient?
- ☐ Are important factual and numerical claims supported by identifiable
  citations or footnotes?

---

### 6.2 Early Project Ambition
A strong proposal should communicate its main ambition early. The evaluator
should ideally understand: (1) What is the central problem? (2) What will the
project fundamentally change? (3) For whom? (4) Through what broad approach?
(5) With what intended level of improvement?

Check:

- ☐ Is the central problem clearly named?
- ☐ Is the main project ambition immediately understandable?
- ☐ Is the intended transformation clear?
- ☐ Are the principal users or beneficiaries identifiable?
- ☐ Is the broad solution concept understandable?
- ☐ Are major quantitative ambitions included where meaningful?
- ☐ Is the ambition sufficiently strong for Horizon Europe?
- ☐ Is the ambition credible within the project duration, consortium capacity and
  Type of Action?

Classify the overall ambition as: Under-ambitious · Appropriately ambitious ·
Highly ambitious but credible · Over-ambitious / credibility concern.

---

### 6.3 Project-at-a-Glance Figure
Check whether an early figure communicates the project concept.

- ☐ Is there an early project-at-a-glance or overall concept figure?
- ☐ Is it located sufficiently early to help the evaluator understand the project?
- ☐ Does it show relevant elements such as: the problem or need; overall project
  concept; Key Technologies; Key R&I Developments or Key Innovations; main users;
  main methodological stages; validation settings; intended transformation?
- ☐ Is it informative rather than decorative?
- ☐ Can an evaluator understand the basic project concept quickly?

The visual quality of the extracted figure should be assessed through Claude Vision.

---

### 6.4 Narrative Flow
Check whether the Excellence section follows a coherent logic. A strong flow may
follow: Problem → Evidence → Existing Approaches → Limitations → Gap → Target
Users → Objectives → Key Technologies → Key R&I Developments / Key Innovations →
Methodology → Validation.

- ☐ Does each section logically lead to the next?
- ☐ Are major concepts introduced before being used?
- ☐ Is unnecessary repetition avoided?
- ☐ Is terminology consistent throughout Excellence?

---

## 7. Problem, Need and Project Rationale

### 7.1 Problem Definition
- ☐ Is the central problem clearly defined?
- ☐ Is the problem sufficiently specific?
- ☐ Is it sufficiently important for a Horizon Europe project?
- ☐ Is it not so broad that the project cannot realistically address it?
- ☐ Are affected users, organisations, sectors, systems, communities or
  scientific fields identifiable?
- ☐ Are current consequences of the problem explained?
- ☐ Is the need for further research and/or innovation convincingly established?

### 7.2 Problem Evidence
- ☐ Is the problem supported by relevant evidence?
- ☐ Are important numerical claims referenced?
- ☐ Are important scientific claims referenced where necessary?
- ☐ Are claims concerning current performance or limitations supported?
- ☐ Are relevant market, sector, policy or regulatory claims referenced where
  appropriate?
- ☐ Are externally derived baseline values supported?
- ☐ Are references sufficiently recent where recency matters?

### 7.3 Citation and Footnote Presentation
- ☐ Are citations or footnotes consistently presented?
- ☐ If footnotes are used, are they numbered consistently?
- ☐ Are important claims clearly linked to the corresponding citation or footnote?
- ☐ Are there paragraphs containing several external claims but only one unclear
  reference?
- ☐ Are important numerical claims left without an identifiable supporting source?
- ☐ Are footnotes readable?
- ☐ Are important claims supported within the proposal rather than relying only on
  external hyperlinks?

The review should not externally verify whether the cited source itself is correct.

### 7.4 Specific Unresolved Gap
- ☐ Is the unresolved gap explicitly stated?
- ☐ Is the gap more specific than the overall problem?
- ☐ Does the proposal clearly distinguish between: Problem → Existing Approach →
  Limitation → Remaining Gap → Need for the Project?
- ☐ Does the proposed project logically follow from the identified gap?
- ☐ Is the identified gap sufficiently important to justify the proposed R&I effort?

### 7.5 Problem–Solution Proportionality
- ☐ Is the proposed solution proportionate to the problem?
- ☐ Is the project attempting to solve too many independent problems?
- ☐ Can the central project challenge be expressed clearly?
- ☐ Is the scope manageable?
- ☐ Is the number of research questions manageable?
- ☐ Is the number of technologies manageable?
- ☐ Is the number of validation settings manageable?
- ☐ Is the project ambitious without becoming implausible?

Potential warnings include: excessive number of technologies developed from
scratch; excessive number of unrelated research questions; excessive number of
large TRL jumps; too many unrelated application contexts; too many validation
settings without a common methodological rationale; major dependency on
insufficiently defined data; major dependency on insufficiently defined
infrastructure; excessive integration complexity; validation scope
disproportionate to the project duration.

---

## 8. End Users and Target Groups

The main users and target groups should be understandable early in the project logic.

- ☐ Are the primary end users clearly identified?
- ☐ Are secondary users clearly identified where relevant?
- ☐ Are beneficiaries distinguished from actual users?
- ☐ Is it clear who directly interacts with or uses the proposed results?
- ☐ Is it clear who indirectly benefits from the proposed results?
- ☐ Are the main needs of the relevant users explained?
- ☐ Are user needs connected to the identified problem and project rationale?
- ☐ Are user needs reflected in the objectives or methodology?
- ☐ Are user roles clear within validation activities?
- ☐ Are end users involved in relevant R&I activities where appropriate?
- ☐ Is the relationship between the problem, target users and proposed solution clear?

Preferred logic: Problem → Affected Users / Target Groups → User Needs → Gap →
Objective → Proposed Capability → Validation.

---

## 9. Objectives

### 9.1 Number of Major Objectives
Internal preferred range: **4–6 major objectives**.

- ☐ Is the number of major objectives manageable?
- ☐ Are objectives unnecessarily fragmented?
- ☐ Are objectives excessively broad?

Internal guidance: 4–6 preferred; 7–8 review for fragmentation; more than 8
structural warning unless strongly justified; 1–3 review for excessive breadth.
This is an internal proposal-quality heuristic and not an official Horizon Europe
eligibility rule.

### 9.2 Objective Categorisation
Where useful, assess whether objectives are logically categorised. Possible
categories: Scientific · Technological · Societal / Human-Centred · Validation ·
Interoperability / Standardisation.

- ☐ Does the categorisation improve project logic?
- ☐ Does the categorisation reflect the actual project structure?
- ☐ Is there an appropriate balance between scientific, technological and other
  relevant dimensions?
- ☐ Is the proposal excessively technology-oriented where the topic requires
  broader scientific, societal or human-centred transformation?

### 9.3 Objective Title Quality
Each major objective should preferably use a clear action-oriented title
(To develop… / establish… / validate… / investigate… / integrate… / quantify… /
enable… / assess… / demonstrate…).

- ☐ Does the objective title begin with a clear action?
- ☐ Does the title describe an intended achievement rather than merely naming a topic?
- ☐ Is the naming convention consistent across objectives?
- ☐ Is the title concise?
- ☐ Is the title sufficiently project-specific?

Weak: *"Objective 2 — Artificial Intelligence"*. Stronger: *"Objective 2 — To
develop and validate an explainable AI-based decision-support framework"*.

### 9.4 Objective Description Quality
Recommended structure: *By the end of the project, [ACTION] [SPECIFIC RESULT OR
CAPABILITY] for [TARGET USER / CONTEXT] through [MAIN APPROACH], achieving
[QUANTIFIED TARGET OR IMPROVEMENT] compared with [BASELINE OR REFERENCE], and
validating the result across [DEFINED SETTING OR SCALE].*

- ☐ What will be achieved? For whom? In what context? Through what main approach?
  With what measurable target? Compared with what baseline or reference? By when?
  Where will the result be validated?
- ☐ Is the description sufficiently project-specific?
- ☐ Is it sufficiently detailed?
- ☐ Does it remain an objective rather than becoming a Work Package description?

### 9.5 Objective Description Completeness
For every major objective: Why necessary? Which gap? What specific capability,
result or advancement? Who uses/benefits? What measurable change? How verified?
Which project activities deliver it?

### 9.6 Numerical Expressions
For every important numerical target: is the metric clearly defined? Is the unit
clear? Is the baseline clear? Is the comparison condition clear? Is the target
clear, credible, sufficiently ambitious, achievable, measurable during the
project? Is the target rationale identifiable?

Example: *"Increase efficiency by 40%."* The reviewer should determine: what is
meant by efficiency; compared with what; what is the baseline; how the 40% will be
measured; in what validation setting; why 40% is credible.

## 10. SMART Assessment

Every major objective should be assessed individually.

- **10.1 Specific** — precise result? clear scope? clear target context?
  identifiable users? avoids generic wording?
- **10.2 Measurable** — relevant KPIs? quantitative targets? objectively
  verifiable? measurement approach identifiable?
- **10.3 Attainable** — achievable within duration? consistent with starting
  maturity, resources, consortium capabilities? data/infrastructure available? TRL
  progression realistic?
- **10.4 Relevant** — addresses an identified gap? pertinent to the topic?
  contributes to overall ambition? necessary rather than peripheral?
- **10.5 Time-Bound** — linked to a defined timeframe? achievement point
  identifiable? verification point identifiable?

Overall SMART Status: ✅ Fully SMART · ⚠️ Partially SMART · ❌ Not SMART.

---

## 11. KPIs and Baselines

- **11.1 KPI Relevance** — at least one meaningful KPI per objective; directly
  measures achievement; measures performance/effect/achievement rather than only
  activity.
- **11.2 KPI Baseline** — baseline defined? numerical where appropriate? source
  identifiable (current operational performance, existing system/product,
  scientific benchmark, previous project, historical dataset, baseline study). If
  the baseline is established during the project, is this stated? Flag: *percentage
  improvement claimed without a defined baseline*.
- **11.3 KPI Target Rationale** — target justified (literature, benchmark, current
  performance, previous prototype, end-user requirement, regulatory/operational
  threshold)? Does it appear arbitrary, too easy, or unrealistically ambitious?
- **11.4 KPI Measurement Completeness** — for each important KPI can you identify:
  metric, baseline, target, unit, measurement method, validation setting, timing,
  responsible activity?

---

## 12. Objective Traceability

Each objective should be traceable to: KPI, Baseline, Target, Key Technology,
Key R&I Development / Key Innovation, WP, Task, Validation Setting, Expected Result.

Preferred logic: Objective → KPI → Baseline → Target → Key R&I Development / Key
Innovation → Key Technology → WP → Task → Validation Setting → Expected Result.

- ☐ Do the objectives collectively cover the whole project?
- ☐ Is unnecessary overlap avoided?
- ☐ Does each objective have a distinct role?
- ☐ Are important project activities missing from the objective structure?
- ☐ Are there objectives without identifiable implementation activities?
- ☐ Are objectives arranged in a logical sequence?

---

## 13. Key Technologies

### 13.1 Identification
- ☐ Are the principal Key Technologies explicitly identified?
- ☐ Is the number manageable?
- ☐ Is each technology genuinely important to the project?
- ☐ Is each linked to one or more objectives?
- ☐ Is each linked to a meaningful R&I purpose?
- ☐ Is each linked to one or more Key R&I Developments or Key Innovations?

### 13.2 Key Technology Naming Quality
Assess whether Key Technology names are strong, distinctive, project-specific,
technically meaningful, memorable and credible. Generic labels flagged where used
as the complete identity of a major Key Technology: Web/Mobile Application, Web
Platform/Console, Dashboard, Database, AI Module, LLM, Digital Twin, Decision
Support System. The problem is not the component's existence but using a generic
delivery format or broad category as the *complete identity* of a major Key
Technology.

- ☐ Strong and distinctive name? Project-specific? Communicates the actual
  technological capability? More specific than a generic category? Technically
  meaningful? Memorable? Helps establish a strong project identity? Credible given
  the methodology? Used consistently throughout?

Examples — Weak: *LLM* → Stronger: *Domain-Tuned Disaster Expert Agent*. Weak:
*AI Module* → Stronger: *Context-Aware Multi-Source Risk Reasoning Engine*. Weak:
*Digital Twin* → Stronger: *Adaptive Cross-Infrastructure Resilience Twin*. Do not
mechanically rename every generic component — this applies primarily to
technologies presented as major Key Technologies or strategic building blocks.

### 13.3 Key Technology Name Credibility
A strong name must not exaggerate actual capability. Flag where the technology has
a distinctive name but the described methodology does not justify the claimed
positioning. Preferred principle: Strong + Distinctive + Project-Specific +
Technically Justified.

### 13.4 Key Technology Description
For each major Key Technology: What is it? What problem does it address? What
specific role? What inputs/outputs? Which components does it interact with? Which
objective does it support? Which Key R&I Development / Key Innovation does it
enable? Starting maturity? Target maturity? How validated?

---

## 14. Key Research and Innovation Developments — RIA

For an RIA, identify and assess the principal Key R&I Developments. For each: What
unresolved question/uncertainty/limitation does it address? What new
knowledge/method/model/capability/experimental evidence is generated? Why a
meaningful R&I advance? How does it advance beyond the State of the Art? Which Key
Technologies enable it? Which objectives does it support? How validated? What
maturity advancement? Forms of advancement: scientific, methodological,
technological, knowledge-based, experimental, proof-of-feasibility.

---

## 15. Key Innovations — IA

For an IA, identify and assess the principal Key Innovations. For each: What
currently exists? What limitation remains? What specifically changes? Practical
added value? Measurable improvement expected? Enabling Key Technologies? Supported
objectives? How demonstrated? Maturity advancement?

### 15.1 Key Innovation Naming Quality
Names should be strong, distinctive, project-specific, meaningful, memorable and
technically credible. Generic examples: AI-Based Innovation, Innovative Platform,
Integrated Solution, Advanced Decision Support, Digital Twin Innovation, Smart
Platform. Weak: *AI Decision Support* → Stronger: *Uncertainty-Aware Multi-Source
Decision Intelligence*.

### 15.2 Key Innovation Name Credibility
Flag inflated terminology, exaggerated AI/autonomy claims, and names implying
capabilities not supported by the methodology. A strong name must remain
evidence-based.

---

## 16. Key Technology ↔ Key R&I Development / Key Innovation Mapping

- ☐ Is every Key R&I Development / Key Innovation linked to identifiable enabling
  Key Technologies?
- ☐ Is every major Key Technology linked to a meaningful R&I purpose?
- ☐ Is the relationship explicitly explained (enables / supports / provides data
  to / integrates with / provides functionality to)?
- ☐ Is the mapping consistent across objectives, methodology, architecture, WPs,
  Tasks, validation settings?

Preferred structure: Key R&I Development / Key Innovation → Enabling Key
Technologies → Related Objective → Related WP / Task → Validation Setting.
Potential findings: Key Innovation identified but enabling technologies unclear;
Key Technology mapped differently in architecture vs methodology; major Key
Innovation with no identifiable technical/methodological basis.

---

## 17. Technology vs Innovation

- ☐ Does the proposal distinguish a technology from the actual innovation? Weak:
  *"Our innovation is AI."* Stronger: *"The innovation introduces an
  uncertainty-aware reasoning capability that integrates heterogeneous operational
  data to support context-sensitive decisions."*
- ☐ Does the proposal explain what is genuinely new?
- ☐ Is the novelty more than the integration of standard technologies?

---

## 18. State of the Art

- **18.1 Scientific SotA** — relevant recent literature? seminal works? directly
  relevant approaches? current scientific/methodological limitations? does it
  support the gap?
- **18.2 Technological SotA** — relevant technologies/platforms/frameworks?
  existing capabilities? current technical limitations? relevant standards?
- **18.3 Commercial / Operational SotA** — existing products/services/operational
  approaches objectively described? practical limitations? (Academic-only SotA may
  be insufficient where relevant products or operational solutions exist.)
- **18.4 Previous R&I Projects** — relevant previous/ongoing projects? results
  explained? what is reused vs newly advanced? (Previous Result → Existing Asset →
  Use in the New Project → New Advancement.)
- **18.5 Patent and Publication Search** — described? credible scope? used to
  support novelty?
- **18.6 Existing Approach Comparison** — Existing Approach → Current Capability →
  Limitation → Project Advancement → Expected Advantage. Is the advantage concrete,
  measurable, validatable during the project?
- **18.7 Strength of Advancement** — classify as Incremental · Significant · Highly
  ambitious · Potentially ground-breaking. Is the claimed level supported? Is
  novelty overstated? Appropriate to the Type of Action?

---

## 19. R&I Maturity and TRLs

- **19.1 Overall Maturity** — starting maturity clear? intended end maturity clear?
  appropriate to the Type of Action?
- **19.2 Technology-Specific TRLs** — per major Key Technology: starting TRL stated
  and evidence-supported? target TRL stated and realistic? development/integration
  activities sufficient? validation environment appropriate? (Key Technology →
  Starting TRL → Existing Evidence → Target TRL → End-of-Project Validation.)
- **19.3 TRL Consistency** — component vs system-level TRLs distinguished? TRL
  progression realistic? final validation consistent with claimed target TRL?
  integration maturity considered?

---

## 20. Overall Methodology

- **20.1 Overall Logic** — Requirements → Research → Development → Integration →
  Validation → Refinement. Does the methodology explain how the objectives will be
  achieved? Major inputs/methods/outputs identifiable? Sequence coherent?
- **20.2 Concepts** — principal concepts clearly explained, appropriate,
  consistent with objectives, introduced before use?
- **20.3 Models** — important models explained, role clear, assumptions
  understandable?
- **20.4 Assumptions** — critical assumptions identifiable and credible
  (data, infrastructure, user access, technology maturity)?
- **20.5 Methodological Challenges** — important scientific/technical/integration
  challenges and data limitations identified, with mitigation approaches?

---

## 21. System or Solution Architecture

Where relevant: architecture provided? major components visible? Key Technologies
visible? Key R&I Developments / Key Innovations visible or traceable?
inputs/outputs and data/information flows understandable? interfaces
understandable? users/external systems represented? consistent with methodology?
important components missing? unexplained components present? The extracted
architecture figure should be visually assessed through Claude Vision.

---

## 22. Component-Level Methodology

For each major Key Technology, Key R&I Development or Key Innovation: What is the
input? Method/processing logic? Output? Dependencies? How validated? What
advancement expected?

---

## 23. Data and Critical Inputs

Major required datasets and other critical inputs identified? Source of each major
input clear? Access credible? Quantity/quality sufficient? Real, historical,
synthetic and simulated data distinguished? Major preprocessing explained?
Important data gaps recognised? Fallback approaches considered?

---

## 24. Validation Settings, Use Cases and Demonstrators

- **24.1 Validation Purpose** — per setting: Why included? Which objective / Key
  Technology / Key R&I Development / Key Innovation does it validate? Distinct role?
- **24.2 Validation Context** — application context, environment, users, specific
  need clear?
- **24.3 Current Situation** — current process, existing approaches/tools/systems,
  current limitations, specific challenge explained?
- **24.4 Validation Procedure** — scenario clear? required inputs identified?
  baseline defined? participants/users/samples/environments described? relevant
  KPIs defined? measurement approach understandable? success criteria identifiable?
  feedback/refinement loop included?
- **24.5 Complementarity** — each setting distinct purpose? collectively cover the
  main results? duplication avoided? common validation logic?

---

## 25. European and Transnational Dimension

Where relevant: meaningful European added value beyond having partners from
different countries? meaningful transnational knowledge exchange? cross-country
validation? different regulatory/organisational/contextual conditions considered?
transferability across countries addressed? cross-border relevance visible?

Terminology/positioning check only. Check relevant presence of terms such as:
Co-creation, Human-centric, User-centric, Citizen-centred, Cross-border,
Transnational, Interdisciplinary, Multidisciplinary, Inclusive, Accessible,
Interoperable/Interoperability, Scalable/Scalability, Replicable/Replicability,
Transferable/Transferability, Sustainable/Sustainability, Resilient/Resilience,
Open Science, FAIR, Trustworthy, Explainable, Transparent, Responsible R&I,
Evidence-based, Data-driven, Standardisation, European added value, Stakeholder
engagement, End-user engagement, Participatory approach. Absence of an irrelevant
term should not be penalised.

## 27. Interdisciplinarity

Relevant disciplines identified? Contribution of each explained? Methods from
different disciplines actually integrated? More than merely having partners from
different disciplines? Directly contributes to the methodology? Appropriate
balance between technical and non-technical disciplines?

---

## 28. Social Sciences and Humanities

Where relevant: SSH relevance explained? relevant SSH disciplines identified? SSH
methods meaningfully integrated? SSH contributes beyond communication and
dissemination? Areas: user behaviour, governance, economics, acceptance, ethics,
policy, organisational factors.

---

## 29. Gender Dimension and Gender-Related Considerations

- **29.1 Participation and Equality Measures** — gender balance in consortium /
  leadership / recruitment / studies; Gender Equality Plans; concrete
  equality/non-discrimination measures. These may be positively considered but do
  not automatically demonstrate integration of the Gender Dimension into R&I content.
- **29.2 Gender Dimension in R&I Content** — does the proposal assess whether sex
  and/or gender is relevant to the R&I content? If relevant, is the reason
  explained? If not, is a project-specific justification provided? Where relevant,
  is it reflected in requirements, data collection, design, validation and
  interpretation, and can gender-related findings influence decisions?
- **29.3 Relevant Sex- and Gender-Related Differences** — where materially
  relevant: needs, behaviour, access, adoption, trust, participation, risk
  perception, exposure, vulnerability, physical interaction, ergonomics, safety,
  performance, training needs, use. Not every dimension must be assessed for every
  project.

---

## 30. Inclusivity and Accessibility

Where materially relevant: accessibility, age, disability, digital literacy,
socioeconomic/cultural/linguistic barriers, relevant vulnerable or
underrepresented groups. Do not artificially impose where not materially relevant.

---

## 31. Open Science

- **31.1 Strategy** — project-specific? integrated into the methodology? beyond
  Open Access publications?
- **31.2 Concrete Outputs** — publications, datasets, benchmark datasets,
  software, source code, models, algorithms, workflows, protocols, ontologies,
  taxonomies, knowledge models, open APIs, specifications, methodological
  frameworks, training resources.
- **31.3 Dataset-Level** — per major dataset: what is generated? why valuable? can
  it be shared? restrictions? repository/sharing mechanism? metadata? licensing?
- **31.4 Software, Models, Knowledge Resources** — shared where appropriate?
  restrictions justified?

---

## 32. FAIR Research Output Management

Findable / Accessible / Interoperable / Reusable principles addressed? Metadata,
persistent identifiers, appropriate repositories, standard formats, licensing,
documentation, versioning considered?

---

## 33. Data Management Plan

Is a DMP appropriately planned? Is the timing clear?

## 34. GDPR and Data Governance

Where relevant: is it clear whether personal data are processed? sensitive/special
categories recognised? data minimisation and purpose limitation considered?
anonymisation/pseudonymisation? access controls and data security? retention and
transfers? Are Open Science commitments compatible with data-protection
obligations? Open Science must not be interpreted as requiring personal or
sensitive data to be publicly released.

## 35. Linked National and International R&I Activities

Relevant previous/ongoing projects identified? specific results to be reused
identified? transfer mechanism explained? duplication avoided? existing assets
distinguished from new work? (Previous Result → Existing Asset → Use in the New
Project → New Advancement.)

---

## 36. Tables and Document Structure

For every important table: table number identifiable? meaningful title? referenced
in the main text? clear column headings? units where necessary? understandable
abbreviations? consistent terminology? internally consistent content? does it

## 46A. Core Evaluation Expectations (Primary Scoring Basis)

The Excellence score should primarily assess the quality, ambition, clarity and
credibility of the proposed concept and methodology. These core expectations —
together with the General Scoring Logic bands (`ESR_WRITING_RULES.md` §5) — determine
the score. The numbered checkpoints elsewhere in this rubric are **enhancing/bonus
coverage** for the internal report; their absence or partial status does not on its
own lower the score.

The review should consider:

- Whether the project objectives are clear, relevant, consistent with the call, and sufficiently ambitious beyond the state of the art.
- Whether the problem definition is precise and convincingly connected to the proposed solution.
- Whether the proposal clearly explains the scientific, technical or methodological advance.
- Whether the state of the art is well described and the proposal identifies a credible gap.
- Whether the concept, architecture, models, assumptions and underlying logic are sound.
- Whether the methodology is coherent, feasible, and sufficiently detailed.
- Whether the project's KPIs are measurable, realistic and supported by baselines or justified thresholds.
- Whether key innovations, outputs, technologies or research results are clearly identified and significant.
- Whether the expected TRL is consistent with the action type and call expectations.
- Whether the approach is interdisciplinary where needed, and whether the relevant disciplines are integrated convincingly.
- Whether citizen, stakeholder, end-user or practitioner engagement is meaningful where relevant to the call.
- Whether risks related to technology, data, AI, models, assumptions or validation are recognised and addressed.
- Whether gender, social sciences and humanities, ethics, open science and research data management are properly addressed where relevant.
- Whether figures, terminology and technical descriptions are consistent and help evaluators understand the proposal.

Typical strengths:

- Clear and well-structured objectives.
- Strong alignment with the topic.
- Convincing state-of-the-art analysis.
- Well-defined methodology.
- Credible innovation logic.
- Relevant KPIs with realistic targets.
- Clear validation pathway.
- Strong interdisciplinary integration.

Typical weaknesses:

- Objectives are too broad or mixed with management/dissemination activities.
- KPIs lack baselines.
- State of the art is generic or does not prove novelty.
- Methodology is vague or over-ambitious.
- Terminology is inconsistent.
- Key technologies are described as products rather than research advances.
- Use cases or pilots are not sufficiently connected to the methodology.
- Data sources, assumptions or validation methods are unclear.

## 47. Excellence Scoring

Assign one official score to the complete Excellence criterion. Do not assign
official 0–5 scores to individual sections or items (internal statuses are
diagnostic only).

The score is a **holistic evaluator judgment** against the Core Evaluation
Expectations (§46A) and the General Scoring Logic bands in `ESR_WRITING_RULES.md`
§5 — **never** a tally of ✅/⚠️/❌ across the checklist. The numbered detailed
checkpoints in this rubric are diagnostic coverage / improvement guidance
(**enhancing / bonus**): the absence or `partial` status of an enhancing checkpoint
does **not** on its own lower the score; record such an absent item as `na`
("enhancing — not required"), not `missing`.

Use the General Scoring Logic bands in `ESR_WRITING_RULES.md` §5 (`0.0–5.0`, **finer
increments than 0.5 allowed** — e.g. `4.7`; not restricted to half-points). Score on
whether the **core substance is present and credible** — for Excellence especially:
key technologies / innovations **explicitly and coherently articulated** (a proposal
with no named Key Technologies, or that leaves them scattered/implicit, has a core gap —
do not credit technologies you had to infer/reconstruct), a claimed advance that is
**credible and not over-claimed or self-contradictory**, and **use cases + a validation
strategy clearly explained**, on top of clear call-aligned objectives, a real SotA gap,
and a feasible methodology that explains how the components integrate. Do **not** subtract
for bonus/presentational
gaps (missing tables/figures, MRL/SRL, unbaselined-but-defined KPIs, formatting) or
for **ambition** — appropriate ambition beyond the state of the art is a strength, and
an ambitious but promise-able target is not a weakness. A criterion strong on all core
expectations scores at the top even with several correctable/bonus gaps. But the
**quality** of a core element is itself core: objectives that are not genuinely SMART,
key technologies/innovations named but scattered or not credibly characterised, an
unclear architecture, or a weakly-evidenced advance are **core-quality weaknesses that
lower the score** (not enhancing) — and they **compound**: the more such shortfalls and
the more serious they are, the further Excellence falls. Weigh them holistically and set
the score yourself (see `ESR_WRITING_RULES.md` §1A/§5); do not name only the single worst
issue while leaving the rest out of the score.

## 48. Score–Comment Consistency

Before finalising: Does the written assessment justify the score? Are important
weaknesses reflected? Are only minor shortcomings present for a score close to 5?
Is a score below threshold supported by clearly significant weaknesses? Has the
same weakness been penalised more than once? Are strengths and shortcomings
balanced according to their actual importance?
